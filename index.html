How have landmark educational policies changed the racial makeup of University of California?


For almost 4 decades, maintaining diversity at their campuses has been a top priority for the University of California system. Yet, some landmark policies including the ban of affirmative action in 1996, which were implemented with the intent of fostering more diversity, have only worsened the issue in more competitive campuses while leaving it mostly unresolved in the less competitive schools.

When looking at the historical admissions data system wide and at each individual institution, one fact becomes apparent: educational policies have different effects at institutions of different calibers. Prior to the repeal of affirmative action in California, we see that the racial makeup of competitive institutions, like UC Berkeley, UCLA, and UCSD, to be similar to the racial makeup of California high school graduating classes. In 1996-2000, when the ban on affirmative action went into effect, we saw clear drops in admission rates among African American, Hispanic/Latino(a), and American Indian students in these schools. Over time, we see the rates of Hispanic/Latino(a) students return to their pre-affirmative action levels, but it is likely that this can be attributed to a larger share of Hispanic/Latino(a) people in California. While we do see some recovery around 2001 when UC decided to implement holistic admissions review, we do not see this same recovery among African American and American Indian students.

The most recent major policy change─the removal of standardized testing from applications─seems to show growing rates of admitted students from underrepresented racial groups, however it is still unclear what the long term effects will be on demographic trends. Research also shows that standardized testing scores are key predictors of success in higher education, so increased admission may not necessarily equate to increased success or retention for admitted students across all races.


The Importance of Diversity in Higher Education

Diversity in higher education is extremely important in the movement towards achieving equity across racial groups in the United States. It is a key step in ensuring greater chances of success and socioeconomic mobility for an individual, which is why it is critical for members of marginalized groups to have equitable access to admission at an institution of any caliber and prestige. But diversity is not solely beneficial to students of underrepresented groups; research has shown that students’ exposure to people different from themselves gain cognitive benefits, such as improved critical thinking skills and problem solving ability, which will aid in better navigation of American society and culture in the future. The policy efforts that have been made over the course of the past 3 decades have at times hindered diversity efforts, so it will be interesting to see how recent policy changes and new efforts will promote diversity, especially because it plays a pivotal role in American education.
